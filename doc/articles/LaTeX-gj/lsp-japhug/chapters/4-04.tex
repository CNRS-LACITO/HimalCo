\chapter{Non-finite verbal morphology}

\section{Participles}
Participles are nominalized verb forms that keep some verbal characteristics: they can serve as predicates of subordinate clauses (relative or complement clauses), take TAM, polarity and associated motion marking, and preserve the verb's argument structure.

Participles differ from finite verbs in three ways. First, they cannot serve as the predicate of a main clause. Second, they are not compatible with the personal prefixes and suffixes of the intransitive and transitive conjugations (including direct/inverse marking and past transitive \forme{-t-}, ¬ß XXX).\footnote{Japhug is identical in this regard to Tshobdun and Zbu, but crucially differs from Situ, where nominalized forms in \forme{k…ô-} can bear indexation suffixes (\citet{jackson06guanxiju}, \citet{jacksonlin07}) } Rather, like nouns, they can take a possessive prefix which can be coreferent with one the arguments. Due to the general impossibility of stacking possessive prefixes (¬ß \ref{sec:possessive.paradigm}), at most only one argument can be indexed this way. Third, there are restrictions on TAM marking on participles: they have at most three forms (neutral, perfective and imperfective), and completely lack inferential (¬ß XXX), egophoric (¬ß XXX), sensory (¬ß XXX) and progressive forms (¬ß XXX).

There are three participles in Japhug; the subject S/A participle in \forme{k…Ø-}, the object participle in \forme{k…§-} and the oblique participle in \forme{s…§-}. 

Complex participial forms, including negative, associated motion or TAM prefixes are possible, as shown by example \ref{ex:WGWjAkWqru}. However, never more than four inflexional prefixes are found; forms with all five prefixal slots filled (such as $\dagger$\forme{…Ø-…£…Ø-j…§-k…Ø-qru}) are not accepted by Tshendzin.

\subsection{Subject participles} \label{sec:subject.participles}
The subject participle, built by adding the prefix \forme{k…Ø-} to the verb stem, designates an entity corresponding to the intransitive subject (\ref{ex:kWsi}, ¬ß \ref{sec:absolutive.S} and XXX), a possessor of the subject, or the transitive subject (\ref{ex:WkWndza}, ¬ß \ref{sec:A.kW}, ¬ß XXX) of the base verb. 

 \begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:kWsi}
\gll k…Ø-si  \\
  \textsc{nmlz}:S/A-die \\
 \glt  `The dead one' (many attestations)
\end{exe}

 \begin{exe} 
\ex \label{ex:WkWndza}
\gll …Ø-k…Ø-ndza \\
  \textsc{3sg}-\textsc{nmlz}:S/A-eat \\
 \glt  `The one who eats it.' (many attestations)
\end{exe}

With \forme{a-} initial verbs the \forme{k…Ø-} prefix regularly merge with \forme{a-} as \forme{k…§-}, a form which resembles an object participle (but there is no ambiguity since all \forme{a-} initial verbs are intransitive, ¬ß XXX). For instance, the subject participle of the semi-transitive \japhug{aro}{have} is \forme{k…Ø-…§ro} \ipa{k…§ro} `having, the one who has'.

The subject participle \forme{k…Ø-} prefix is historically related to that of object participles (¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle}), velar infinitives (¬ß \ref{sec:velar.inf}) and deverbal nouns in \forme{x-/…£-} (¬ß \ref{sec:G.nmlz}), and has cognates elsewhere in the family (¬ß \ref{sec:velar.nmlz.history}).

In this section, I discuss first morphological issues (possessive prefixes ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.ambiguities}, other prefixes ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.other.prefixes} and ambiguous forms ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.ambiguities}), and then present the various functions of subject participles, including participial relatives (¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.subject.relative} and ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.other.relative}), complementation strategies (\ref{sec:subject.participle.complementation}), as well as the case of lexicalized participles (¬ß \ref{sec:lexicalized.subject.participle}).
 
Examples which could be potentially be viewed as subject participles in converbial use are analyzed as \forme{k…Ø-} infinitives (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.converb}).

\subsubsection{Possessive prefixes on subject participles}  \label{sec:subject.participle.possessive}

In the case of transitive verbs, a possessive prefix coreferent with the object is obligatory when no overt object is present (\textsc{3sg} \forme{…Ø-} in \ref{ex:WkWndza}), and when no other prefix is added to the participle.

When another prefix (polarity, associated motion or orientation prefix) is present, the possessive prefix is optional, as shown by forms like \forme{m…§-k…Ø-ndza} `the one which does not eat (it)' in (\ref{ex:mAkWndza}), as opposed to \forme{…Ø-m…§-k…Ø-mto} `the one who does not see it' in (\ref{ex:WmAkWmto}) with both possessive \forme{…Ø-} and the negative prefix \forme{m…§-}.

 \begin{exe} 
\ex \label{ex:mAkWndza}
\gll  t…§-mt ∞…Øm  Å…üa  ëo ma n…Ø ma, n…§ki, t…Øjpu m…§-k…Ø-ndza ci tu t…ïe, \\
\textsc{indef}.\textsc{poss}-meat completely \textsc{emph} \textsc{lnk} \textsc{dem} apart.from \textsc{filler} flour.based.food \textsc{neg}-\textsc{nmlz}:S/A-eat \textsc{indef} exist:\textsc{fact} \textsc{lnk} \\
\glt  `There is (an animal like the mouse) which only eats meat, not food made from flour.' (27-spjaNkW, 202-2063)
\end{exe}

 \begin{exe} 
\ex \label{ex:WmAkWmto} 
\gll  li n…Øn…Ø k…Øn…§ …Ø-k…Ø-mto …£…§ ëu, …Ø-m…§-k…Ø-mto …£…§ ëu. \\
again \textsc{dem} also \textsc{3sg}.\textsc{poss}-\textsc{nmlz}:S/A-see exist:\textsc{sens} \textsc{3sg}.\textsc{poss}-\textsc{neg}-\textsc{nmlz}:S/A-see exist:\textsc{sens} \\
\glt `There are (people) who see (find) it, and people who don't.' (20-sWrna, 20)
\end{exe}

In the case of ditransitive verbs, the possessive prefix striclty refer to the object. With indirective verbs like \japhug{t ∞u}{ask}, the possessive prefix is necessarily the theme, never the recipient. The form in (\ref{ex:AkWthu}) thus cannot be interpreted as meaning `the one who asks me (about it)'; the correct construction would be (\ref{ex:ACki.kWthu}), with the recipient in the dative case.

\begin{exe}

\ex \label{ex:ACki.kWthu}
\gll a-…ïki …Ø-k…Ø-t ∞u  \\

\end{exe}

With secundative verbs (¬ß XXX), the possessive prefix of the subject participle is obligatorily coreferent with the recipient, not the theme, as in (\ref{ex:nAkWmbi}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:nAkWmbi}

\end{exe}
With intransitive verbs, including adjectival stative verbs (¬ß XXX), a possessive prefix can also be added. In the case of semi-transitive verbs (¬ß XXX), the possessive can refer to the semi-object (¬ß \ref{sec:semi.object}), as in example (\ref{ex:WkWrga.pWdAn}).

 \begin{exe}

\subsubsection{Associated motion, polarity and orientation prefixes on subject participles}  \label{sec:subject.participle.other.prefixes}
Of all non-finite verb forms, subject participles allow the richest possible combinations of inflexional prefixes: associated motion (¬ß \ref{sec:associated.motion}, example \ref{ex:WCWkWphWt}) below with the translocative \forme{…ï…Ø-}), polarity (¬ß XXX, see \ref{ex:WmAkWmto} above) and orientation prefixes marking TAM (¬ß XXX) all can be prefixed. 
 
\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:WCWkWphWt}
 \gll t…ïeri n…Øra …Ø-…ï…Ø-k…Ø-p ∞…Øt ra k…Ø-tu me ma,   \\

 \glt `But nobody goes to collect (its stalks).' (11-paRzwamWntoR, 90)
\end{exe}

Two of the four series of orientation prefixes are possible with subject participles. With series A prefixes (\forme{t…§-} `up', \forme{p…Ø-} `down' etc, ¬ß XXX), the participle of dynamic verbs is perfective as \forme{t ∞…Ø-k…Ø-…£e} `the one who came' in (\ref{ex:WkWntsGe.thWkWGe}), and takes stem II (¬ß XXX). With series B prefixes (\forme{tu-} `up', \forme{pj…Ø-} `down' etc, ¬ß XXX), it has a habitual imperfective meaning with dynamic verbs as \forme{ju-k…Ø-…£i} `the one who (usually) comes' in (\ref{ex:WkWndza.jukWGi}).\footnote{These two examples also illustrate the use of subject participles as purposive complements with the forms \forme{…Ø-k…Ø-nts…£e} and \forme{…Ø-k…Ø-ndza} (see ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.complementation}, ¬ß XXX).} The prefixes \forme{…≤…Ø-}and \forme{ku-} do appear on subject participles, but only to express imperfective: there are no egophoric (¬ß XXX) or sensory (¬ß XXX) subject partiples.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:WkWntsGe.thWkWGe}
\gll i…ïq ∞a qa ëo …Ø-k…Ø-nts…£e t ∞…Ø-k…Ø-…£e n…Ø …Ø-p ∞e \\
the.aforementioned sheep \textsc{3sg}.\textsc{poss}-\textsc{nmlz}:S/A-sell \textsc{pfv}:\textsc{downstream}-\textsc{nmlz}:S/A-come[II] \textsc{dem} \textsc{3sg}.\textsc{poss}-\textsc{dat} \\
\glt  `(He told) the person who had come to sell the sheep.' (2003kandZislama, 212)
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:WkWndza.jukWGi}
\gll …Ø-k…Ø-ndza ju-k…Ø-…£i n…Ø p…£a ci …≤…Ø-≈ãu \\

\glt   `The one who comes to eat (the fruits) is a bird.' (2012qachGa, 22)
\end{exe}

The participles of stative verbs with series A and B orientation prefixes have an inchoative meaning, exactly like their finite counterpart (¬ß XXX and ¬ß XXX). \end{exe}

\subsubsection{Ambiguities}  \label{sec:subject.participle.ambiguities}
The subject participle \forme{k…Ø-} prefix is homophonous with the generic person marker for intransitive subject and object (¬ß XXX; note that these two prefixes are probably historically related). In the case of intransitive verbs, some subject participles are therefore homophonous with generic person forms. 

For instance, the past imperfective generic \forme{p…Ø-k…Ø-≈ãu} `one used to be' in (\ref{ex:pWkWNu.genr}) is identical to the past imperfective participle \forme{p…Ø-k…Ø-≈ãu} `the one who used to be ....', discussed above (example \ref{ex:pWkWNu} in ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.other.prefixes}). In this example, it is obvious that \forme{k…Ø-} is the generic person marker because the verb \forme{p…Ø-k…Ø-rga} `one used to be' occurs as main verb; outside of any context,  \forme{t…§-p…§tso p…Ø-k…Ø-≈ãu} could be understood as a relative clause `the one who used to be a child', but this is not the meaning of this sentence. 

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:pWkWNu.genr}
 \gll t…ïeri t…§-p…§tso p…Ø-k…Ø-≈ãu t…ïe, n…Ø k…§-ndza wuma  ëo p…Ø-k…Ø-rga. \\
 \textsc{lnk} \textsc{indef}.\textsc{poss}-child \textsc{pst}.\textsc{ipfv}-\textsc{genr}:S/P-be \textsc{lnk} \textsc{dem} \textsc{inf}-eat really \textsc{emph} \textsc{pst}.\textsc{ipfv}-\textsc{genr}:S/P-like \\
 \glt `When (we) were children, (we) used to like eating it.' (12-ndZiNgri, 137-138)
\end{exe}

More generally, the factual, imperfective, past imperfective and perfective forms of intransitive verbs in generic person forms are homophonous with unmarked, imperfective, past imperfective and perfective participles respectively. In the case of transitive verbs, the subject participle can be identical to the object generic form. For instance, the participle \forme{n…Ø-tu-k…Ø-ndza} `the one who eats them' in (\ref{ex:tukWndza.nmlz}) only differs from the generic \forme{tu-k…Ø-ndza} `it eats us/people' in \ref{ex:tukWndza.genr}) by the possessive prefix \forme{n…Ø-}, and that prefix being optional, there are forms that are really ambiguous between participle and generic.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:tukWndza.genr}
 \gll t…ïe ndz…§pri k…§-ti n…Ø t…ïe t…Ørme tu-k…Ø-ndza …≤…Ø-≈ãgr…§l  \\

The irregular generic \forme{tu-k…Ø-ti} `one says' of the verb \japhug{ti}{say} is also identical with the participle `the one who says'.

The \textsc{2sg}\fl{}\textsc{1sg} form of transitive verbs in \forme{-a}, due to the vowel fusion rule \ipa{-a-a} \fl{} \forme{-a}, are also superficially identical to subject participles, for instance \forme{tu-k…Ø-ndza-a} `you eat me' is pronounced \phonet{tuk…Øndza} exactly like the generic and the participle \forme{tu-k…Ø-ndza} in the Kamnyu dialect (in the dialects of Japhug where this vowel fusion does not occur, the forms remain distinct).

In the case of stative verbs and some auxiliary verbs, the infinitive has in some cases the form \forme{k…Ø-}, and there is thus ambiguity between infinitive and subject participial forms for these verbs (¬ß \ref{sec:velar.inf}).

\subsubsection{Subject relative clauses}  \label{sec:subject.participle.subject.relative}
The most common use of subject participles is to build participial relative clauses whose head noun is the subject; it is the only way to relativize the subject in Japhug (¬ß XXX). Headless relatives are most common (¬ß XXX), but when the head noun is overt, the relative can be either prenominal, postnominal or head-internal. With intransitive verbs the difference between postnominal or head-internal relatives is often difficult to ascertain, and many examples are ambiguous; for instance in (\ref{ex:tCheme.RnWz.kWrWCmi}), the relative clause could be argued to be postnominal (limited to the participle \forme{k…Ø-r…Ø…ïmi} `speaking') or head-internal (including \forme{t…ï ∞eme  Ån…Øz} `two girls', and possibly even the previous adjunct).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:tCheme.RnWz.kWrWCmi}
 \gll  k ∞a …Ø-≈ãg…Ø n…Øt…ïu t…ï ∞eme  Ån…Øz k…Ø-r…Ø…ïmi pj…§-tu. \\

\glt  `There were two girls speaking in the house.' (150909 xiaocui-zh, 157)
\end{exe}

With transitive verbs, subject head-internal relatives can be distinguished from postnominal ones  by the presence of the ergative \forme{k…Ø} on the head noun (¬ß XXX), as in (\ref{ex:WrdWrdoR.kW.thotsi.WkWta}).

\begin{exe}

Prenominal relatives are relatively rare with intransitive verbs, but commonly occur with transitive verbs, as in (\ref{ex:tWnW.WkWtshi}). \subsubsection{Complementation strategies}  \label{sec:subject.participle.complementation}
Subject participles are also required in several types of complement clauses and complementation strategies (¬ß XXX on the difference between the two types).

The most common complementation construction involving subject participles occurs with the motion verbs \japhug{…ïe}{go} and \japhug{…£i}{come}. These verbs have purposive clauses which compulsorily take a verb in subject participle form (¬ß XXX).  When the verb in the purposive clause is transitive, the participle has a possessive prefix coreferent with the object as in the case of relative clauses (\ref{sec:subject.participle.possessive}), and the subject can either take absolutive marking following the motion verb (which is morphologically intransitive), as in (\ref{ex:WkWCar.chACe}), or ergative  marking following the verb of the purposive clause as in (\ref{ex:WkWCar.loCenW}). This case marking difference can be analysed as reflecting distinct clausal structures: in (\ref{ex:WkWCar.loCenW}), the subject \forme{t…§-r…üit ra} `the children' belongs to the purposive clauses, whereas in (\ref{ex:WkWCar.chACe}), the subject \forme{t…§-mu n…Ø} lies outside of it.

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:WkWCar.chACe}
 \gll lo-fso Å t…ïe t…ïe t…§-mu n…Ø […Ø-t…ï…Ø …Ø-k…Ø-…ïar] c ∞…§-…ïe t…ïe,\\

\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:WkWCar.loCenW}

\glt  `The morning of the next day, the children went (there) to look for him.'  (Norbzang, 325)
\end{exe}

The goal of the motion verb can however occur within the purposive clause, as in (\ref{ex:khapa.WkWnnAjo}), where the subject in ergative form \forme{…Ø-wa n…Ø k…Ø} `his father' is stranded from the transitive verb \forme{…Ø-k…Ø-n-n…§jo} by the goal \forme{k ∞apa t…ïe} `downstairs'.

Some transitive and semi-transitive verbs take object complement clauses (¬ß XXX) requiring a subject participle. This group includes the verb \japhug{s…Øœás…§l}{recognize, notice} as in (\ref{ex:tAkWnWCpWz.pjAsWXsAl}) and the verbs of pretence \japhug{ ë…£…§pa}{pretend} and \japhug{n…Ø…ïp…Øz}{pretend, disguise as, imitate} as in (\ref{ex:kukWtshi.tonWCpWznW}).

 \gll t…ïe n…Øn…Ø k…Ø [qa ëo t…§-k…Ø-n…Ø…ïp…Øz] n…Ø pj…§-s…Øœás…§l. \\

\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:kukWtshi.tonWCpWznW}
 \gll   ëara k…Ø [c ∞a n…Ø ku-k…Ø-ts ∞i] to-n…Ø…ïp…Øz-n…Ø, \\

First, the three verbs in question can take nouns as objects (as shown by \ref{ex:qa…ïpa.tonWCpWz} and \ref{ex:tAkWnWCpWz.pjAsWXsAl}) instead of clauses with subject participles.

\subsection{Object participles} \label{sec:object.participle}
The object participle is a nominalized form which refers to an entity corresponding to the object (\ref{sec:absolutive.P}) or semi-object (¬ß \ref{sec:semi.object}) of the base verb. All transitive and semi-transitive verbs (except for \japhug{k…§t…Øpa}{tell}, ¬ß XXX) can build an object participle by adding the prefix \forme{k…§-} (for instance \forme{k…§-ndza} from the verb \japhug{ndza}{eat} in \ref{ex:kAndza}). This form is homophonous with, and historically related to the velar infinitive (¬ß \ref{sec:velar.inf}, ¬ß \ref{sec:velar.nmlz.history}).

 \begin{exe} 
\ex \label{ex:kAndza}
\gll k…§-ndza \\
   \textsc{nmlz}:P-eat \\
 \glt  `The one that is eaten.' (many attestations)
 \end{exe}

In the case of secundative verbs (¬ß XXX), the object participle can either refer to the recipient or the theme, as in (\ref{ex:nWkAmbi}); this question is discussed in more detail in ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle.relatives}.

  \begin{exe} 
\ex \label{ex:nWkAmbi}
\gll n…Ø-k…§-mbi \\
   \textsc{pfv}-\textsc{nmlz}:P-give \\
 \glt  `The one that he has given it to.'
 \glt `The one that has been given to him.'  (many attestations)
 \end{exe}

   
 In this section, I first describe the morphological properties of object participles (compatibility with possessive prefixes ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle.possessive} and other prefixes ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle.other.prefixes}). Then, I discuss several cases of ambiguity between object participles and other \forme{k…§-} prefixed forms in ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle.ambiguity} (see also ¬ß \ref{sec:infinitives.participles}). The uses of object participles to build relative clauses and complement clauses are described in  ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle.relatives} and ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participles.complement}. Finally, I present a few cases of lexicalized object participles in ¬ß \ref{sec:lexicalized.object.participle}.

\subsubsection{Object relative clauses} \label{sec:object.participle.relatives}
Object participles can be used to build object relative clauses, but compete in this function with finite relatives (¬ß XXX). They differ in this regard from subject relatives, which are the only available construction to relativize transitive and intransitive subjects.

\section{Infinitives}

\subsection{Velar infinitives} \label{sec:velar.inf}
The most common infinitives in Japhug are the velar infinitives, build from the stem I of the verb and prefixed either with \forme{k…§-} or or \forme{k…Ø-}; they are homophonous with participles and not always easily distinguishable from them (¬ß \ref{sec:infinitives.participles}). They are in particular the preferred citation form of the verbs (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.citation}), though not with all speakers.
 
The \forme{k…Ø-} infinitives are found with stative verbs (including adjectives and existential verbs), impersonal modal verbs and some anticausative verbs (¬ß XXX); other verbs take the \forme{k…§-} infinitives. 

Stative verbs in \forme{a-} have regular fusion of \forme{k…Ø-} and \forme{a-} as \ipa{k…§-}, and thus superficially appear to have \forme{k…§-} infinitives (for instance, the infinitive of \japhug{ar≈ãi}{be green} is \forme{k…Ø-…§r≈ãi} \ipa{k…§r≈ãi}).

\subsubsection{Infinitives vs participles} \label{sec:infinitives.participles}
It is not immediately obvious that a category of `velar infinitives' needs to be distinguished from participles in Japhug, as both are non-finite verbal categories prefixed in \forme{k…§-} or \forme{k…Ø-} (¬ß \ref{sec:object.participle.ambiguity} and ¬ß \ref{sec:subject.participle.ambiguities}). 

The necessity to set \forme{k…§-} infinitives apart from object participles stems from the fact that the latter can only be built from transitive or semi-transitive verbs, while the former also occurs with strictly intransitive verbs. Thus, if one were to argue that all \forme{k…§-}prefixed non-finite forms of transitive verbs are object participles, including in the case of complement clauses (for instance \forme{k…§-ndza} in \ref{ex:kAndza.mWpWrYota}), one would not be able to account for the \forme{k…§-}prefixed forms of intransitive verbs occurring in the same context such as \forme{k…§-…ïe} in (\ref{ex:kACe.mWpWrYota}) -- even though \japhug{…ïe}{go} could be considered to be an indirect transitive verb (since it can take a goal, which can be relativized with a finite relative clause, ¬ß XXX), it is not possible to build a participial relative clause by prefixing \forme{k…§-} on this verb (the oblique participle \forme{s…§-} must be used instead, ¬ß \ref{sec:oblique.participle}).

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:kAndza.mWpWrYota}
\gll a ëo k…§-ndza m…Ø-p…Ø-r…≤o-t-a \\
\textsc{1sg} ???-eat \textsc{neg}-\textsc{pfv}-experience-\textsc{pst}:\textsc{tr}-\textsc{1sg} \\
\glt `I never ate that.' (many attestations)
\end{exe}

\begin{exe}
\ex \label{ex:kACe.mWpWrYota}
\gll  aj k…§-…ïe m…Ø-p…Ø-r…≤o-t-a \\
\textsc{1sg} \textsc{inf}-go \textsc{neg}-\textsc{pfv}-experience-\textsc{pst}:\textsc{tr}-\textsc{1sg}  \\
\glt `I never went there.' (150820 ZNGWloR, 4)
\end{exe}

I therefore adopt the following criteria to discriminate between a \forme{k…§-} infinitive and an object participle:  \forme{k…§-} non-finite forms of (non-semi-transitive) intransitive verbs are infinitives; \forme{k…§-} non-finite forms of transitive and semi-transitive verbs occurring in the same contexts as the infinitives of intransitive verbs are infinitives.

By systematically applying these criteria, we can identify three contexts where infinitives are attested: citation form (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.citation}), complementation (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.complementation}; there are however a few cases of object participles used in complement clauses, ¬ß \ref{sec:object.participles.complement}), and manner converbs (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.converb}). 

Distinguishing between subject participles and \forme{k…Ø-} infinitives in Japhug is less straightforward, unlike in other Gyalrong languages such as Tshobdun for instance, \citealt{jackson14morpho}, since even stative verbs take the \forme{k…§-} infinitive in complement clauses (¬ß \ref{sec:kW.infinitive}). The only clear contexts where \forme{k…Ø-} infinitives do occur is that of citation forms (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.citation}) and complement clauses containing impersonal modal verbs (¬ß \ref{sec:kW.infinitive}). Converbs in \forme{k…Ø-} are analyzed as infinitives rather than subject participle because they are only attested with stative verbs or other verbs taking the \forme{k…Ø-} infinitives (¬ß \ref{sec:inf.converb}).

\subsubsection{Ambiguity}  \label{sec:velar.inf.ambiguity}
Aside from the homophony between infinitives and participles discussed in ¬ß \ref{sec:infinitives.participles}, another type of ambiguity occurs with verbs selecting the orientation prefix `east', whose A form is \forme{k…§-} (¬ß XXX). Intransitive verbs in imperative singular and perfective third singular forms (for instance \forme{k…§-r≈ãg…Ø} `he laid down') and transitive verbs without stem alternation in imperative singular (\forme{k…§-ts ∞i} `drink!', ¬ß XXX) have forms that are homophonous with the corresponding infinitives ( \forme{k…§-r≈ãg…Ø} `to lie down', \forme{k…§-ts ∞i}  to drink'), but these cases are never really ambiguous, as it is trivial to distinguish between a finite verb form and a non-finite one, for instance by changing from singular to dual or plural. 

For instance, in a particular context, if a form such as \forme{k…§-r≈ãg…Ø} can be changed to the corresponding plural \forme{k…§-r≈ãg…Ø-n…Ø} (which can be either perfective \textsc{pfv}-lie.down-\textsc{pl} `they laid down' or imperative `lie down!'), it is possible to conclude that this \forme{k…§-r≈ãg…Ø} is necessarily finite (since non-finite verb forms in Japhug never take indexation affixes, ¬ß XXX) and cannot be an object participle or an infinitive.

\subsubsection{Converbial use}    \label{sec:inf.converb}
